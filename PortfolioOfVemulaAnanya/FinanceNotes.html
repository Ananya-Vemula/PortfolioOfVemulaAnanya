Personal Finance Fundamentals

Budget

A plan that helps you manage your money, encompassing income, spending, savings, and more.

Monthly Budget Components (After Tax Reduction):

  • Total Income: All money received from various sources (salary, freelance, investments, etc.) after taxes are deducted.
  • Savings: Money set aside for future goals.
  • Wants: Discretionary spending on non-essential items or experiences.
  • Needs: Essential expenses required for living.

General Budgeting Rule (50/30/20 Rule):

  • Needs: 50% of your after-tax income
  • Wants: 30% of your after-tax income
  • Savings: 20% of your after-tax income

What Comes Under Savings:

  • Emergency Fund: A crucial safety net, ideally covering 3-6 months of essential living expenses. This fund should be easily accessible but separate from your everyday spending money.
  • For Expensive Purchases: Saving for significant planned expenses like a down payment on a house, a car, or a major vacation.
  • Investment and Long-Term Funds: Money allocated for wealth growth over time, such as retirement accounts, education funds, or general investment portfolios.

Credit Score

A numerical representation of your creditworthiness, indicating how likely you are to repay borrowed money on time. It's akin to a GPA for lenders and recruiters.

Important Note: Your income has no direct bearing on your credit score.

How to Improve Your Credit Score:

  • Pay Bills on Time: This is the most critical factor. Late payments significantly damage your score. Consider setting up automatic payments.
  • Credit Utilization: Keep your credit utilization ratio low (ideally below 30%). This means using a small percentage of your available credit. For example, if you have a credit limit of $10,000, try to keep your balance below $3,000.
  • Credit History: The longer your history of good money habits (responsible use of credit), the better. Avoid closing old, unused credit accounts, as this can shorten your credit history.
  • Type of Credit (Credit Mix): Having a mix of different credit types (e.g., credit cards, installment loans like mortgages or car loans) can positively impact your score, showing you can manage various forms of credit responsibly.
  • New Cards/Inquiries:
    • Hard Inquiry: Occurs when you apply for new credit (e.g., a new credit card, loan). These can temporarily ding your score, especially if you have too many in a short period.
    • Soft Inquiry: Occurs when you check your own credit score or when a lender pre-approves you for an offer. These do not affect your credit score.

Credit Cards

Credit cards can offer significant benefits like rewards (cash back, travel points), purchase protection, and are excellent tools for building and maintaining a good credit score. However, they require careful management to avoid falling into debt due to high interest rates.

  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The annual rate charged for borrowing, or the annual rate of interest paid on money earned. This is the total interest and fees charged on an amount owed over a year, divided by the average balance owed.
  • Grace Period: The period during which no interest is charged on new purchases if you pay your entire balance by the due date. This typically lasts 21-25 days from the statement closing date. Paying your balance in full every month allows you to avoid paying interest.

S.M.A.R.T. Goals

A framework for setting effective goals that are more likely to be achieved.

  • Specific: Clearly defined, not vague. (e.g., "Save $5,000" instead of "Save money")
  • Measurable: Quantifiable, with criteria for tracking progress. (e.g., "Save $500 per month")
  • Achievable: Realistic and attainable given your resources and constraints.
  • Realistic: Relevant to your life and overall objectives.
  • Time-bound: Have a specific deadline or timeframe for completion. (e.g., "Save $5,000 by December 31st")

Investment

Putting money into assets with the expectation of generating a return or appreciation over time. It's crucial for achieving long-term financial goals like retirement.

Net Worth = Assets - Liabilities

  • Assets: What you own (e.g., cash, investments, real estate, cars).
  • Liabilities: What you owe (e.g., loans, credit card debt, mortgages).

Loans

Borrowing money from an individual or a financial institution, with an agreement to pay it back with a specific interest rate over a set period.

  • Installment Credit: A type of loan where you borrow a fixed amount of money and pay it back in regular, equal installments over a predetermined period (e.g., car loans, mortgages, student loans).
  • Revolving Credit: A type of credit that allows you to borrow up to a certain credit limit, pay back a portion, and then borrow again. Interest is charged on the outstanding balance. (e.g., credit cards).
  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The true cost of borrowing money annually, including interest and other fees. It's calculated as: (Total interest and fees in a year) / (Average balance owed).

Good Debt vs. Bad Debt (Generated by ChatGPT)

  • Good Debt: Typically involves borrowing for assets that are likely to increase in value or generate income, thus improving your financial position in the long run. Examples include:
    • Mortgages: A loan to purchase real estate, which often appreciates over time and provides a place to live.
    • Student Loans: Investing in education can lead to higher earning potential and career advancement.
    • Business Loans: Funding a business that has a strong potential for growth and profitability.
  • Bad Debt: Involves borrowing for depreciating assets or consumption, where the borrowed money does not generate a return and often comes with high interest rates. Examples include:
    • High-Interest Credit Card Debt: Especially when used for everyday expenses or impulse purchases. The interest accrues quickly, making it difficult to pay off.
    • Payday Loans: Extremely high-interest, short-term loans often used by individuals in urgent need of cash, leading to a cycle of debt.
    • Loans for Depreciating Assets: Taking out a loan for luxury items that lose value quickly, such as certain consumer electronics or vehicles (beyond basic transportation needs).

Debt Repayment Approaches:

  • Debt Snowball Method: You pay off your smallest debt first, regardless of interest rate, while making minimum payments on all other debts. Once the smallest is paid off, you roll that payment into the next smallest debt. This method provides psychological wins, keeping you motivated.
  • Debt Avalanche Method (High Rate Approach): You prioritize paying off the debt with the highest interest rate first, while making minimum payments on all other debts. This method saves you the most money on interest over time.

Insurance

A contract where an individual or entity receives financial protection or reimbursement against losses from an insurance company. It's a way to manage risk.

  • Insured: The individual or entity covered by the insurance policy.
  • Insurer: The company that provides the insurance coverage.
  • Agent: A licensed professional who helps you purchase insurance policies and make informed choices.

Types of Insurance:

  1. Medical Insurance (Health Insurance): Covers medical expenses, prescription drugs, and sometimes dental and vision care. It protects you from high healthcare costs.
  2. Property Insurance: Protects your physical assets from damage or loss due to events like fire, theft, or natural disasters. This includes homeowner's insurance and renter's insurance.
  3. Car Insurance (Auto Insurance): Provides financial protection against physical damage or bodily injury resulting from traffic collisions and against liability that could also arise from incidents in a vehicle.
  4. Life Insurance: Provides a financial payout to your beneficiaries upon your death, offering financial security to your loved ones.
  5. Term Insurance: A type of life insurance that provides coverage for a specific period (the "term"). If you die during the term, your beneficiaries receive a death benefit. It's generally more affordable than whole life insurance.

Insurance Key Terms:

  • Premium: The regular payment you make to the insurer to keep your insurance policy active.
  • Deductible: The amount of money you must pay out-of-pocket for covered expenses before your insurance coverage begins to pay.
  • Co-pay: A fixed amount you pay for a covered healthcare service after you've paid your deductible. Your insurance then covers the rest.
  • Policy Limit: The maximum amount the insurer will pay for a covered claim within a specific policy period.
  • Benefit: The payment the insurer will make to cover what the claim is for, according to the terms of the policy.

Saving vs. Investing

It's generally better to start investing early due to the power of compound interest, which allows your money to grow exponentially over time.

  • Saving: Securely storing money, keeping it easily accessible, minimizing risk, and ideally growing it slightly over time (e.g., in a savings account). The primary goal is preservation and liquidity.
  • Investing: Putting money into assets like stocks, bonds, or mutual funds with the goal of significant long-term growth. It involves accepting higher risk for the potential of greater returns.

It's best to diversify investments to mitigate risk.

Regularly reviewing and adjusting your financial plan (monthly is ideal) is crucial.

Types of Investments:

  • Low Risk, Low Return:
    • Money Market Accounts: Savings accounts that offer slightly higher interest rates than traditional savings accounts but may have some restrictions on withdrawals.
    • Treasury Bills (T-Bills): Short-term debt securities issued by the U.S. government, considered very safe.
    • Bonds: Debt securities issued by governments or corporations. They pay regular interest payments and return the principal at maturity. Generally less volatile than stocks.
  • Moderate Risk, Moderate Return:
    • Mutual Funds: A professionally managed portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities. Diversification is built-in.
    • Index Funds: A type of mutual fund or ETF that tracks a specific market index (e.g., S&P 500). They offer broad market exposure and typically have lower fees.
  • High Risk, High Return:
    • Single Stocks: Investing in individual companies. Offers the potential for significant gains but also significant losses.
    • Cryptocurrency: Digital currencies like Bitcoin or Ethereum. Highly volatile and speculative.
    • Commodities: Raw materials or primary agricultural products such that they can be bought and sold (e.g., gold, oil, agricultural products). Prices can be very volatile.

Scams and Frauds

  • "If it's too good to be true, then it's too good to be true": Be highly skeptical of offers that promise unusually high returns with little to no risk.
  • Stranger Danger: Be cautious about sharing personal or financial information with unknown individuals or suspicious websites. Verify the legitimacy of requests for information. Common red flags include unsolicited emails, calls, or messages asking for personal details, or pressure to act immediately.

Career and Education

  • Consider the Return on Investment (ROI) when making decisions about education and career paths. This means evaluating the potential financial benefits (higher salary, job security) against the costs (tuition, time spent not working).
  • Factor in all associated costs: Transportation, housing, food, personal expenses, and educational materials.
  • Financial Aid: Scholarships, grants, and low-interest loans can significantly reduce the cost of education. Explore all available options.
  • Opportunity Cost: This refers to the value of the next best alternative that was not taken. When you are paying for education, the opportunity cost includes the income you could have earned if you were working instead of studying, in addition to the direct costs of tuition and fees.
  • A master's degree doesn't automatically guarantee higher salary expectations for everyone. Research salary trends in your chosen field and location to determine if the investment is worthwhile.

Taxes

Mandatory contributions levied by a government on the income, consumption, and wealth of individuals and businesses to fund public services.

  • Flat Taxes: A tax system where everyone pays the same percentage of their income, regardless of how much they earn (e.g., sales tax on receipts, hotel taxes, VAT).
  • Progressive Tax: A tax system where individuals with higher incomes pay a higher percentage of their income in tax (e.g., income tax).
  • Deductibles (Tax Deductions): Amounts that can be subtracted from your gross income, reducing your taxable income. They do not directly reduce the amount of tax you owe, but rather the amount of income on which your tax is calculated.
  • Tax Credits: Directly reduce the amount of tax you owe, dollar for dollar. A $1,000 tax credit reduces your tax bill by $1,000. These are generally more valuable than deductions.

Types of Accounts

  • Checking Account: An everyday transactional account designed for frequent deposits and withdrawals. They typically offer low or no interest rates and are highly liquid.
  • Money Market Accounts (MMAs): A type of savings account that often offers higher interest rates than standard savings accounts but may have certain limitations, such as a minimum balance requirement or a limited number of transactions per month.
  • Certificates of Deposit (CDs): A type of savings account that holds a fixed amount of money for a fixed period, and in return, the issuing bank pays interest. They offer good returns but impose a penalty for early withdrawal.
  • Investment Accounts:
    • Stock Accounts: Accounts used to buy and sell individual stocks.
    • Special Investment Retirement Accounts (e.g., 401(k), IRA): Tax-advantaged accounts designed specifically for retirement savings, often with employer contributions.
    • Brokerage Account: A general investment account that allows you to buy and sell a wide range of investments, including stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs.

Inflation

The general rise in the price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time, leading to a decrease in the purchasing power of money.

Inflation makes your money worth less over time. This is why simply "doing nothing" with your money by letting it sit idle in a low-interest savings account can be just as detrimental as reckless spending, as its purchasing power erodes.

Housing

  • In some situations, renting a home can be cheaper than buying, especially in high-cost-of-living areas or if you only plan to stay in a location for a short period. Renting offers flexibility and fewer responsibilities for maintenance and repairs.
  • Long-term, buying a house is often a better choice for wealth building if you are settled in life and do not anticipate moving frequently. Homeownership can build equity, offer potential tax benefits, and provide a sense of stability. However, it comes with significant financial commitments and responsibilities.
